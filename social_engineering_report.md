# Social Engineering Attacks

## Introduction

Social engineering is a cyberattack technique that manipulates people into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise security. Instead of exploiting software vulnerabilities, attackers exploit human trust, curiosity, fear, or lack of awareness.

---

## 1. Phishing

### Overview
Phishing is one of the most common social engineering attacks. Attackers send fraudulent emails, messages, or websites pretending to be legitimate organizations.

### Impact
- Theft of login credentials
- Financial fraud
- Identity theft
- Data breaches

### Prevention
- Verify sender identities
- Avoid clicking suspicious links
- Use multi-factor authentication
- Report suspicious emails

---

## 2. Pretexting

### Overview
Pretexting involves creating a fabricated scenario to convince a victim to provide sensitive information.

### Example
An attacker may pretend to be an IT support employee and request account credentials.

### Impact
- Unauthorized access
- Information disclosure
- Financial losses

### Prevention
- Verify identities before sharing information
- Follow organizational security procedures
- Limit disclosure of sensitive data

---

## 3. Baiting

### Overview
Baiting attacks attract victims using tempting offers or infected devices.

### Example
An attacker leaves an infected USB drive labeled "Confidential Data" hoping someone will connect it to a computer.

### Impact
- Malware infections
- System compromise
- Data theft

### Prevention
- Avoid unknown storage devices
- Follow security policies
- Educate employees on cyber risks

---

## Real-World Impact

Social engineering attacks have caused significant financial and reputational damage to organizations worldwide. Many successful cyberattacks begin with human manipulation rather than technical exploitation.

---

## Conclusion

Social engineering remains one of the most effective attack methods because it targets human behavior. Security awareness, employee training, and strong verification procedures are essential for reducing these risks.
